For Medical Providers

Guidelines for writing effective accommodation letters while protecting your patients' privacy rights

Your Role in Workplace Accommodation

As a healthcare provider, you play a crucial role in helping pregnant patients and new parents secure necessary workplace accommodations. Your medical documentation can be the key to ensuring your patients maintain their health, job security, and legal rights during this important time.

This guide will help you understand what information to include in accommodation letters, what employers can legally request, and how to protect your patients from inappropriate inquiries about their medical information.

Writing Effective Accommodation Letters

Essential Information to Include

  • Patient's name and your professional relationship to them
  • Statement that the patient has a medical condition related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions
  • Specific accommodation recommendations (e.g., modified duties, schedule changes, seating, frequent breaks)
  • Duration of the accommodation need (temporary, ongoing, specific dates)
  • Your professional credentials and contact information
  • Date of the letter and your signature

Information You Should NOT Include

  • Specific medical diagnosis or detailed medical history
  • Detailed descriptions of symptoms or medical procedures
  • Information about fertility treatments or pregnancy complications
  • Personal medical details unrelated to the accommodation request
  • Prognosis or detailed medical predictions
  • Information about mental health conditions unless directly relevant

Sample Accommodation Letter

Template for Professional Use

[Date]

To Whom It May Concern:

I am writing as the treating physician for [Patient Name]. Ms. [Last Name] is under my medical care for conditions related to pregnancy.

Based on my medical evaluation, I recommend the following workplace accommodations to ensure her health and safety:
• [Specific accommodation, e.g., "Modified work schedule with frequent breaks"]
• [Additional accommodations as needed]

These accommodations are medically necessary and should remain in place through [date/duration].

Please contact me at [phone number] if you require clarification regarding these accommodation recommendations.

Sincerely,
[Your name and credentials]
[Practice name and contact information]

Protecting Patient Privacy

⚠️ Invalid Employer Requests

Employers may not legally request detailed medical information, specific diagnoses, or personal health details beyond what is necessary to understand the accommodation need. These requests violate both HIPAA privacy rules and employment discrimination laws.

What Employers Can Legally Request

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and New York Human Rights Law, employers may only request:

  • Confirmation that a medical condition exists
  • Information about functional limitations that require accommodation
  • Suggestions for reasonable accommodations
  • Expected duration of the accommodation need

When Employers Overstep Boundaries

If an employer requests inappropriate medical information such as:

  • Detailed medical records or test results
  • Specific pregnancy complications or medical history
  • Information about fertility treatments
  • Personal details about the patient's condition
  • Prognosis or detailed medical predictions

You should not provide this information. Instead, advise your patient that such requests may be discriminatory and suggest they contact Family Beginnings for legal guidance.

When Your Patient Needs Legal Support

If an employer is requesting inappropriate medical information or denying reasonable accommodations, refer your patient to Family Beginnings for free legal consultation.

Key Legal Protections

Your patients are protected under several important laws:

  • New York Human Rights Law: Prohibits pregnancy discrimination and requires reasonable accommodations
  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA): May apply to pregnancy-related medical conditions
  • Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA): Provides job-protected leave for qualified employees
  • New York Paid Family Leave: Provides paid leave for bonding with new children
